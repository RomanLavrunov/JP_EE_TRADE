01 — Ask

Defining the research question

Business context

A mid-sized Estonian trading company began working with a Japanese supplier in late 2019 — coinciding with the EU-Japan EPA coming into force. The company's strategy focuses on distant markets where logistics complexity creates a natural barrier for smaller competitors. The EPA reduced that barrier significantly.

South Korea went through a similar process earlier — EU-Korea EPA came into force in 2011. Does the Korean experience offer a preview of Japan's trajectory? And can such patterns be extrapolated to the EU-India FTA currently under negotiation?

Research questions

  • Did Estonia's trade with Japan increase after the EPA — and how did exports and imports behave separately?
  • Which commodity groups grew in the 2019-2022 window, before geopolitical disruption set in?
  • How does the Estonia-Korea post-2011 pattern compare to Estonia-Japan post-2019?
  • How did COVID-19 and the war in Ukraine affect the data?
  • Can findings tentatively apply to the EU-India trade agreement?

How the commodity groups were selected

The raw data contains all 99 two-digit CN codes. Most have zero or suppressed values for Estonia–Japan trade throughout the entire period. Three sequential filters reduced this to the 10 groups used in the analysis.

Filter 1

Minimum total volume

Groups where cumulative exports over the full period fell below a minimum threshold were dropped. This eliminated ~80 codes with no meaningful Estonia–Japan trade history.

Filter 2

Continuity across years

Groups with non-zero values in fewer than 7 of the 11 analysis years were excluded. This removed one-off transactions that don't represent a structural trade relationship.

Filter 3

Valid baseline year (heatmap only)

The heatmap index requires a 2018 value to calculate value / value_2018 × 100. CN08 Edible fruit & nuts has a suppressed value (".." → NA) specifically in 2018 — the trade happened to be zero or below the reporting threshold that year. The group is excluded from the heatmap only; it appears normally in other charts.

Filter 4

Direction check

CN32 Paints and CN48 Paper passed the volume threshold — but Estonia is a buyer from Japan in these categories, not a seller. Export values were 150–250 thousand EUR/year with no trend, so they were excluded from the export analysis.

The absence of a group is as meaningful as its presence. CN32 and CN48 are not in the export charts because Estonia is a buyer, not a seller, in those categories. CN08 is present in time series and slope charts — but absent from the heatmap specifically because its 2018 value is suppressed, making the index undefined for that year. The group itself shows consistent growth and is a real export relationship.

04a — Analyze · Japan dynamics

Bilateral trade overview

The EPA year (2019) brought a visible export uptick — exports averaged 51 M€/year in the post-EPA period vs 36 M€ pre-EPA, a real and measurable shift. The real surprise came in 2022: trade hit an all-time peak at 150 M€, driven by surges in electrical machinery and chemicals. Then it fell sharply and kept falling.

The cause is logistics. Russia's airspace closure in February 2022 eliminated a routing that cut freight transit times by 3–4 hours on a round trip. That sounds minor until you consider that air cargo to Japan is already at the edge of what makes economic sense for many goods. Doubling cargo times made some shipments unviable. Maritime alternatives add 4–6 weeks. No other major Estonian trade partner is as exposed to this as Japan — the distance simply doesn't matter as much for nearby markets.

The import side tells a different story. It's structurally stable, dominated by machinery and electronics, with no dramatic EPA effect visible. Estonia buys from Japan fairly consistently regardless of trade agreements.

04b — Analyze · Partner comparison

Japan vs other trade partners

The growth index chart puts Japan in perspective. Kazakhstan and Bulgaria show higher growth numbers than Japan — but these are geopolitical artifacts. Kazakhstan became a transit corridor for goods rerouted around Russia's sanctions regime. Bulgaria's growth is tied to defence procurement flows linked to the war. Neither tells you anything useful about trade agreements.

Singapore is a more honest comparison: also EPA 2019, also a distant market — and it didn't grow like Japan either. That means the EPA alone doesn't explain Japan's trajectory. The commodity mix matters. The logistics route matters. The timing matters.

South Korea remains the most relevant benchmark. The EU–Korea EPA came into force in 2011, and its effect took 3–5 years to fully show up in trade data. Japan is now at year 5–6. The trajectory isn't necessarily finished — but it requires the logistics situation to stabilise before the EPA's tailwind can reassert itself.

04c — Analyze · Commodity structure

What actually grew — and what didn't

CN44 Wood is roughly half of all Estonian exports to Japan — not because of the EPA, but because of Japan's long-standing demand for Nordic and Baltic timber. The agreement gave an existing relationship an extra push (+40% on the post-EPA average), but it didn't create it. This distinction matters: the EPA amplified what was already there.

CN85 Electrical machinery is a cleaner signal. Growth of +58% on a real base (9.2 M€ → 14.6 M€) is a genuine post-EPA story — these are products where tariff reduction directly changes the competitive economics. CN28 Inorganic chemicals shows even steeper growth (+95%), though the base is smaller.

CN84 Machinery looks dramatic at +426% — but the pre-EPA base was 0.6 M€. That's roughly 30–60 machines per year at typical unit values. A trend worth watching, but not yet a major story.

The counter-cases are just as informative. Fish exports fell 48% post-EPA. Dairy barely moved. The EPA removes tariffs — it doesn't remove competition from other EU exporters who have the same access. Where Estonia isn't already competitive on quality, proximity, or price, lower tariffs don't change the outcome.

04d — Analyze · External shocks

COVID, war, and logistics

COVID-19 caused a brief dip in 2020 — smaller than expected. Wood exports proved resilient, which makes sense: construction timber demand didn't collapse the way consumer discretionary spending did. The pandemic is visible in the data but not dramatic.

The war is a different story. The closure of Russian airspace in February 2022 hit Japan harder than almost any other trade partner, for one simple reason: distance. Air cargo to Japan was already operating near the edge of economic viability for many product categories. The Russian airspace route shaved 3–4 hours off a round trip — enough to make the difference between viable and marginal for some shipments. After closure, round-trip cargo times roughly doubled; maritime alternatives add 4–6 weeks and change the economics entirely.

This is the key confounding factor in reading the post-2022 data. The EPA is still in force. Tariffs are still lower. But the logistics infrastructure that would allow exporters to take advantage of those lower tariffs is under structural strain that has nothing to do with the trade agreement.

There's an uncomfortable inversion here: the same logistics barriers that are suppressing overall trade volumes are simultaneously raising the competitive moat for those who have already built the capability to navigate them. Declining volumes mean fewer active competitors, not a worse market — for the right type of goods.

05 — Share

Visualisation choices

Each chart was chosen to answer a specific question rather than illustrate the same data in different formats.

  • Line chart (bilateral) — the baseline view. 2019 uptick, 2022 peak, post-2022 decline visible in one glance. Structural breaks labelled so the context is on the chart, not in surrounding text.
  • Growth index (2019 = 100) — the comparative view. Japan as a red line against a grey pack of other countries. The key question — did Japan outperform after EPA? — is answerable in about five seconds.
  • Treemap — 2023 export structure in a single snapshot. CN44's dominance (~40%) is immediately visible without reading a table.
  • Slope chart — how groups moved across five key years. Shows relative shifts better than overlapping lines on a shared time axis.
  • Heatmap — all groups × all years at once. Green above 2018 baseline, red below. Patterns invisible in individual charts become apparent.
  • Pre/post-EPA bar — % change per commodity group, signed. Growth and decline visible in the same view.
  • Regional stacked bars — Japan in the context of Estonia's full export geography for Wood and Fish. For Wood, Japan is the dominant non-European buyer. For Fish, Japan is a thin slice at the top of a Europe-dominated stack.

All charts are built with Chart.js from JSON files generated by the R scripts. Updating the dashboard requires only re-running the R scripts and uploading new JSON files — no changes to HTML or JS.

06 — Act

Conclusions

EPA worked — moderately

Post-EPA average exports +40% vs pre-EPA (36 M€ → 51 M€/year). Real growth in wood, chemicals (+95%), electrical machinery (+58%). Consistent with policy intent.

Logistics beats tariffs post-2022

Russia's airspace closure hit Japan harder than any other major partner. Trade peaked in 2022 then declined even as the EPA remained in force.

Japan ≠ Asia

South Korea grew while Japan declined post-2022. Distance, routing, and freight infrastructure are country-specific — treating Asian markets as one block leads to wrong conclusions.

The Korea precedent

EU-Korea EPA (2011) took 3-5 years to show full effect. Japan is at year 5-6 — the trajectory may not be final, but requires logistics to stabilise first.

Counter-cases are real

Fish exports fell 48% post-EPA. Dairy flat. The EPA removes tariffs — it does not remove competition from other EU suppliers.

Current conditions = selective opportunity

Declining volumes mean reduced competition for those who can navigate the logistics barrier. Niche, high-value, non-time-sensitive goods are best positioned.

India: use Japan as a baseline, not a guarantee

EU-India FTA in negotiation. Japan's experience — slow start, sector-specific, logistics-dependent — is a useful baseline. India presents even greater distance challenges.

The EPA worked — trade grew in the right sectors and in the right direction. But "working" and "transformative" are different things. Japan remains a small but strategically interesting partner, and the current combination of lower tariffs and higher logistics barriers may create better entry opportunities than the headline numbers suggest — for exporters who can operate at that distance.
